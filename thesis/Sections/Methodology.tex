\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
The methodology for this thesis project stands on the development of a drone to use for the various tests and implementations, together with a controlled environment to test the drone in.
The development consists of hardware choices, software choices, and the constraints arising from these two, which dictate much of the design.
\subsection{Requirements and Constraints}
\subsubsection{Flight Type}
A quadcopter design was a very clear choice as these are widely used and allow for very nimble and precise movement as opposed to fixed-wing designs which need to be in forward motion to generate upthrust for their flight. The Quadcopter design due to its symmetric nature makes it easy to control predictably.

\autoref{fig:drone_movement} illustrates a standard quadcopter setup. If front motors ($m_1$, $m_2$) spin comparatively more, the drone pitches up; if side propellers ($m_1$, $m_4$) spin comparatively faster, the drone rolls towards its left side. For yaw, the alternating spin-direction layout is exploited: propellers diagonal from each other ($m_1$ and $m_3$) spin faster while $m_2$ and $m_4$ spin slower, inducing rotation in the direction of the faster propellers via Newton's first law.
Opposingly, pitch and roll is achieved by spinning pairs of propellers on one side faster than on the other (left vs right, front vs back).
This makes the control of quadcopter somewhat simple as it can be summarized in a mathematical model.

\subsubsection{Constraints, Design Objectives, and Platform Sizing}
This work is a continuation of the Spinfly platform by A.~Lerche \cite{lercheSpinFlyUAVSystem}, which identified two primary limitations that directly shaped this project: \textbf{insufficient weight headroom}---the original platform struggled with takeoff when equipped with the protective cage, leaving no margin for component upgrades---and \textbf{insufficient battery capacity}, which created operational friction by limiting test throughput.

These problems are coupled: battery mass scales with capacity, yet a heavier battery forces higher motor output, accelerating discharge and potentially negating the added capacity. Solving both simultaneously required systematic trade-off analysis. Lerche also noted a lack of robust autonomous control and unreliable telemetry, which informed the autopilot selection.

From these constraints, three design targets were formulated:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Carry larger payloads} --- sufficient headroom for the cage and future instrumentation.
    \item \textbf{Achieve longer flight duration} --- extend mission time while maintaining efficient power draw.
    \item \textbf{Simplify autonomous control} --- adopt a proven, modular flight controller to reduce software complexity.
\end{enumerate}

The drone's physical dimensions are governed by a narrow three-way tradeoff, illustrated in \autoref{fig:drone_sizing_tradeoff}:

The 4-inch wheelbase is the upper limit for confined indoor flight while providing sufficient thrust to carry the companion computer and protective cage.}

\textbf{Lower bound: payload minimum.} The Raspberry~Pi~5 companion computer ($\sim$60\,g plus peripherals) and the PETG (polyethylene terephthalate glycol, a 3D-printing filament) protective cage (35.8\,cm diameter, bearing assembly) together require a minimum thrust budget that class-3-inch and below platforms cannot provide. A typical 3-inch build lacks both the stator diameter and the propeller disc area to lift these components while maintaining a safe TWR (thrust-to-weight ratio) margin.

\textbf{Upper bound: indoor safety ceiling.} The 4-inch propeller class is the practical upper limit for indoor flight in confined spaces.\footnote{As recommended by commercial test platforms: \url{https://vibms.com/what-size-fpv-drone-should-i-get/}} Larger platforms (5-inch and above) produce propeller wash that destabilises the vehicle in tight enclosures and increase the kinetic energy carried into any collision, raising the barrier to physical survivability. Platforms large enough to carry heavy sensors such as a full-size LIDAR (e.g., Ouster OS0 at $\sim$450\,g) exceed the thrust budget while also exceeding safe indoor operating dimensions.

\textbf{The 4-inch optimum.} At this scale, 2004-stator motors paired with 4-inch GF4023 propellers on a 6S battery produce sufficient lift for the companion computer and cage while hovering near the efficiency peak. Small drones of this class are rarely required to carry the combined payload of a companion computer, protective cage, and bearing mechanism; the target all-up weight was 1,200\,g (projected TWR $\sim$2.87:1), and the final assembly settled at approximately 1,340\,g (actual TWR $\sim$2.57:1; detailed derivation in \autoref{sec:annex_hardware}). The most powerful 2004-stator motors available for 4-inch propellers were therefore selected. Despite the lower-than-projected thrust headroom, this margin proved sufficient to arrest post-collision drift and recover to the commanded setpoint.

To ensure self-righting after ground impacts, researchers have developed various passive recovery structures. Mulgaonkar et al.~\cite{mulgaonkarRobustAerialRobot2018} demonstrated the efficacy of a 25~g G\"omb\"oc-shaped shell for autonomous uprighting. Conversely, Mahmud~\cite{mahmudDevelopmentCollisionResilient2021} investigated rotating-shell principles, comparing a single-axis turtle-shell design against a 3-axis gimbaled truncated icosahedron. The rotating cage configuration adopted for this thesis takes direct inspiration from Mahmud's rotating-shell principle, adapting the mechanism specifically for airborne impact deflection rather than just ground recovery.

The development process began with CAD modelling of the frame and cage assembly in OnShape, iterating through several revisions to balance structural rigidity, weight, and propeller clearance.

The bearing is integrated into the frame's structural core: the inner race is fixed to the frame plates, while the outer race carries the PETG shell. \subsubsection{Motor and Battery Selection}

The design targets a \textbf{thrust-to-weight ratio (TWR) of approximately 2.87:1} (at the design weight of 1,200\,g; actual assembly settled at $\sim$1,340\,g for a TWR of $\sim$2.57:1), positioning the platform in the research/autonomous zone where hover occurs at roughly 40\% throttle. This mid-throttle hover is deliberate: motor efficiency peaks at 40--70\% throttle, and hovering at 50\% reserves substantial headroom for autonomous obstacle avoidance, gust rejection, and post-collision recovery. Hovering at or near 100\% throttle leaves zero control margin.\cite{bershadsky2016propulsion}

\textbf{EMAX ECOII 2004 1600KV} motors were selected to meet this target. The 2004 stator (20\,mm diameter, 4\,mm height) provides high torque at mid-throttle while the low 1600\,KV rating (1600\,RPM per volt) is matched to a \textbf{6S LiPo battery} (22.2\,V nominal). On this voltage, the motors deliver approximately 860\,g static thrust each under 4-inch GF4023 propellers---a combined 3,440\,g~\cite{bershadsky2016propulsion}\footnote{Product specifications available at: \url{https://drone24hours.com/products/t-motor-f2004-1700kv}}. The 6S voltage also places the practical RPM ($\sim$24,000--28,000 under load) within the 4-inch propeller's efficient range, avoiding excessive tip speeds.

\textbf{Efficiency Advantages of the 6S Architecture.}
The choice of a 6-cell battery over a 4-cell alternative is driven by efficiency. Theoretical foundations of brushless DC (BLDC) motor dynamics demonstrate that higher-voltage architectures (such as 6S) are significantly more efficient than lower-voltage alternatives (such as 4S) for equivalent power outputs. Because electrical power is proportional to voltage multiplied by current ($P = VI$), increasing the system voltage proportionally reduces the current required to generate the same mechanical thrust. This reduction in current quadratically decreases the resistive copper losses ($P_{\text{loss}} = I^2R$) within the motor windings and electronic speed controllers, thereby reducing thermal dissipation and extending overall flight endurance.\cite{hughes2013electric}


\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,

\textbf{Voltage Sag.}
Voltage sag is the temporary voltage drop that occurs under high-current discharge, caused by battery internal resistance ($V_{\text{drop}} = I \times R_{\text{internal}}$). Under an equivalent current spike, a 4S pack may sag by over 30\% of its nominal voltage, whereas an equivalent-quality 6S pack sees a smaller relative drop---not necessarily because the absolute sag in volts is smaller, but because the same sag is a smaller \emph{fraction} of the higher baseline voltage.\cite{bershadsky2016propulsion} For autonomous flight, where the controller expects a predictable motor response, excessive sag introduces a latency between commanded and actual thrust that degrades control stability during rapid maneuvers. Higher baseline voltage provides a larger operating margin against this effect. The detailed derivation is given in the \hyperref[sec:annex_hardware]{Annex}.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Flight Controller: PX4 and Pixhawk 6C}

The flight controller occupies the Hardware \& Firmware layer of the system architecture (\autoref{fig:architecture}), directly interfacing with the motors, ESCs, and onboard sensors. To address the three design objectives holistically, PX4 was selected as the autonomous platform. Two platforms currently dominate the open-source UAV ecosystem: ArduPilot and PX4. PX4 was chosen over ArduPilot for its permissive BSD licensing (which allows proprietary extensions, whereas ArduPilot's General Public License version 3 (GPLv3) requires contributed code to remain open), its modular publish-subscribe architecture (uORB), and its well-developed hardware standards under the Dronecode Foundation~\cite{meierPX4NodebasedMultithreaded2015}. PX4's open-source standard is built on modular architecture using \textbf{uORB (microObject Request Broker)}---a publish-subscribe middleware layer suited for plug-and-play development. These factors promised faster integration with the ROS~2 middleware layer and reduced hardware-software troubleshooting. This modularity enables rapid research iteration: novel autonomous behaviors can be implemented as independent modules without modifying the core flight stack.

The \textbf{Pixhawk 6C flight controller} was chosen as the hardware instantiation of the PX4 standard. Critically, Pixhawk is not a monolithic design but represents standardized connector specifications, sensor integration points, and reference schematics managed by the Dronecode Foundation~\footnote{\url{https://www.unmannedsystemstechnology.com/2023/01/latest-pixhawk-open-standards-available/}}. This standardization means:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hardware swappability}: Sensors and peripheral electronics can be upgraded or replaced with components from different manufacturers (Holybro, ARK Electronics, CUAV) without redesigning integration.

    \item \textbf{Ecosystem maturity}: Over 1 million Pixhawk-based devices are deployed across commercial, research, and hobbyist applications.\footnote{\url{https://www.unmannedsystemstechnology.com/2023/01/latest-pixhawk-open-standards-available/}}

    \item \textbf{Sensor integration}: The Pixhawk 6C includes integrated IMU and compass. Specifically, it features redundant inertial measurement units with onboard ICM-42688-P and BMI088 accelerometers/gyroscopes, plus an IST8310 magnetometer, with temperature-controlled operation via onboard heating resistors.\footnote{Software documentation available at: \url{https://docs.px4.io/main/en/flight_controller/pixhawk6c}} This eliminates the need for custom sensor fusion code---allowing focus on the research question rather than flight control tuning.
\end{itemize}

The 6C's form factor (84.8 \(\times\) 44 \(\times\) 12.4 mm) justifies the 4-inch drone design choice.\footnote{\url{https://docs.holybro.com/autopilot/pixhawk-6c/technical-specification}} Four-inch platforms represent the upper practical limit for indoor flight while remaining large enough to carry meaningful payloads.

The Pixhawk 6C runs PX4 Autopilot firmware (\texttt{v1.16.1rc}) and is responsible for low-level attitude control, motor mixing, and sensor fusion. Its dual IMU sensors (ICM-42688-P and BMI088) and onboard IST8310 magnetometer are fused in the EKF2 estimator alongside external vision odometry from the MoCap system. The magnetometer is disabled for indoor operation to prevent interference with MoCap yaw data. The full parameter configuration is given in the repository \texttt{README.md}.

\subsubsection{Companion Computer: Raspberry Pi 5}

The companion computer is a \textbf{Raspberry Pi 5} (8\,GB variant), selected for its substantial compute headroom sufficient for concurrent MoCap data ingestion, offboard control computation, and high-bandwidth telemetry logging.

The Pi~5 runs the flight software stack inside a \textbf{Docker container} provisioned with Ubuntu~22.04 LTS and ROS~2 Humble (full provisioning details in the \hyperref[sec:appendix_architecture]{Software Annex}). The container communicates with the Pixhawk~6C over a dedicated UART serial link as shown in \autoref{fig:architecture} and \ref{fig:architecture_xrce_dds}.

The Pi~5 communicates with the flight controller over UART (TELEM2) and mounts directly above it within the 4-inch frame envelope.

\subsubsection{Communication Middleware Between Flight Controller and Pi~5}

The communication middleware (Micro-XRCE-DDS) acts as a serial bridge, translating the flight controller's internal C++ struct messages (uORB) into standard ROS~2 network messages (\autoref{fig:architecture_xrce_dds}). During experimental flights, \textbf{uXRCE-DDS} is used exclusively: the PX4 uXRCE-DDS client publishes internal uORB topics to the ROS~2 node graph via \texttt{MicroXRCEAgent} running on the Pi~5. A secondary \textbf{MAVLink} stream can be enabled for ground-station debugging (QGroundControl), but the two protocols are not used simultaneously during active data collection.

\subsubsection{MoCap--PX4 Coordinate Frame Integration}
\label{sec:coordinate_frames}

The \texttt{motion\_capture\_tracking} ROS~2 node receives the multicast stream from Motive, and the \texttt{mocap\_px4\_bridge} node transforms the poses between coordinate frames before publishing them to \path{/fmu/in/vehicle_visual_odometry}.

Coordinate frame conventions differ between MoCap and PX4. ENU is a ground-fixed frame where the $X$~axis points East, $Y$~points North, and $Z$~points Up; the vehicle body frame has $X$~toward the front, $Z$~up, and $Y$~toward the left. PX4's internal state estimator operates in the \textbf{NED (North-East-Down)} frame, where $X$~points North, $Y$~points East, and $Z$~points Down; the corresponding vehicle body frame (\textbf{FRD}, Forward-Right-Down) has $X$~toward the front, $Z$~down, and $Y$~accordingly. \autoref{fig:coordinate_frames} shows both conventions side by side.\footnote{Software documentation available at: \url{https://docs.px4.io/main/en/tutorials/motion-capture}}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/coordinate_frames_NEDvsENU.png}
    \caption{NED (left) and ENU (right) coordinate frame conventions. Adapted from PX4 documentation~\footnote{Software documentation available at: \url{https://docs.px4.io/main/en/tutorials/motion-capture}}.}
    \label{fig:coordinate_frames}
\end{figure}

The \texttt{mocap\_px4\_bridge} performs this transform in two steps: first, a static rotation maps ENU to NED ($X_{\text{NED}} = Y_{\text{ENU}}$, $Y_{\text{NED}} = X_{\text{ENU}}$, $Z_{\text{NED}} = -Z_{\text{ENU}}$); second, the NED pose is rotated into the FRD body frame using the vehicle's current attitude quaternion. The resulting \texttt{VehicleVisualOdometry} message is published at 120\,Hz to \path{/fmu/in/vehicle_visual_odometry}.

The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF2) estimator fuses this external vision with the onboard 250\,Hz IMU data. The fidelity of this sensor fusion under both normal and degraded tracking conditions is validated later in this section and in \autoref{sec:motion_capture}, where the EKF velocity estimates are shown to align closely with MoCap-derived ground truth and to bridge transient marker dropouts during impact via inertial propagation.

\paragraph{The Flight Director}

The \texttt{flight\_director.py} node was developed directly from the PX4 ROS~Com offboard control examples, inheriting their structure for heartbeat publishing, offboard mode switching, and trajectory setpoint generation. It serves as the primary mission executor:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State machine}: Implements the waypoint state machine shown in \autoref{fig:flight_director_state_machine} and detailed in \autoref{fig:mission_geometry}. Each state transition publishes a waypoint-identifier message that drives the event segmenter.
    \item \textbf{Offboard control}: Publishes \texttt{TrajectorySetpoint} messages to \path{/fmu/in/trajectory_setpoint} with explicit velocity feedforward (\texttt{hb.velocity = True}). Subscribes to \path{/fmu/out/vehicle_status} for telemetry feedback.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Safety Enforcement}

The flight director enforces a multi-layered safety regime designed to prevent the drone from leaving the OptiTrack capture volume under any flight condition.

\textbf{Geofence boundary.} Before each experimental session, a geofence polygon is defined and loaded into the flight director. This boundary is deliberately drawn \emph{inside} the physical extents of the 6-camera OptiTrack Prime~22 capture volume, ensuring that the drone cannot exit the tracking area even at maximum excursion. The boundary is inflated inward by the cage radius (17.9\,cm) plus an additional safety margin, so that a geofence breach is triggered before any part of the cage can contact an external obstacle or leave the tracked volume. If the drone's MoCap position crosses this boundary at any time, the flight director immediately commands an emergency landing.

\subparagraph{Sensor Fusion and Redundancy Safeguards}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Geofence/MoCap loss failsafe.} Before flight, the flight director verifies that all planned waypoints (including the collision sweep and recovery path) project safely inside the geofence boundary with the cage-radius offset subtracted. During flight, if the OptiTrack tracking rate drops below a critical threshold---indicating marker occlusion or tracking degradation---or the drone's MoCap position crosses the geofence, the flight director immediately commands an emergency landing.

    \item \textbf{Transient occlusion during impact.} A distinct and more nuanced problem arises during the exact millisecond of physical impact: the protective cage and column structure physically block the infrared camera's line-of-sight to the drone's reflective markers. This transient marker occlusion causes the MoCap tracking data to glitch. When velocity and acceleration are derived from position by numerical differentiation, even a single-frame position glitch produces a massive, non-physical spike in the resulting kinematic profile. The Savitzky-Golay differentiation filter cannot remove this artefact---it amplifies any discontinuity in the underlying data. Figure~\ref{fig:mocap_dropout} illustrates this failure: the MoCap-derived velocity and acceleration traces exhibit violent, unphysical excursions at the impact instant ($t = 0$), while the EKF2 odometry remains smooth and continuous throughout the event.

    \item \textbf{EKF2 inertial bridging.} The flight controller's EKF2 does not rely solely on the external vision stream. It continuously fuses the onboard 250\,Hz IMU accelerometer and gyroscope data into its state estimate. When the MoCap feed momentarily degrades during impact, the inertial propagation bridges the gap without introducing noticeable drift. The smooth, physically plausible EKF velocity profile through the collision window---visible in Figure~\ref{fig:mocap_dropout}---confirms that the filter successfully compensates for the optical dropout.

    \item \textbf{EKF2--MoCap validation.} Figure~\ref{fig:ekf_validation} overlays the EKF-estimated velocity against the MoCap-derived velocity for representative flights from both cage configurations during clean, steady flight. The close alignment between the two traces confirms that the EKF2 produces velocity estimates consistent with the external reference. Based on this validation, EKF odometry is adopted as the primary source of velocity and acceleration data for all kinematic calculations through the collision window. The raw MoCap position data is retained solely for geometric reference (impact angle computation, clearance measurement, and trajectory visualisation)---calculations that do not require numerical differentiation and are therefore insensitive to transient position glitches.

    \item \textbf{Kill switch.} A spacebar-activated software kill switch runs in the flight director's main loop, providing the operator with an instant disarm capability independent of the geofence and MoCap monitors.
\end{itemize}
