\section{Experiments and Results}

The Results chapter presents a deliberately structured progression from raw mechanical measurements to inferential analysis. Each plot represents a deliberate analytical choice: the figures are ordered to first establish the baseline dataset and spatial context, then characterise the physical dynamics of impact (deceleration, vibration, trajectory deviation), and finally assess whether the onboard IMU signatures alone can recover the impact angle without external MoCap ground truth. Comparative metrics between the Fixed Cage and Rotating Cage configurations are presented side-by-side throughout, enabling direct visual and statistical comparison of the bearing mechanism's effect.

To evaluate the operational and protective capabilities of the drone cage and to assess what indication of impact angle might be recoverable from onboard sensors, physical collision tests were conducted. This section details the experimental setup, the software pipeline used to capture the data, and the direct mechanical results, comparing the performance of the fixed cage against the freely rotating cage.

\subsection{Motion Capture Setup}
\label{sec:motion_capture}

External state estimation is provided by a \textbf{6-camera OptiTrack Prime~22} system streaming at 120\,Hz via the Motive software platform (Mudfish network middleware). The Prime~22 offers sub-millimetre positional accuracy at short range, sufficient for the $\sim$5\,m$\times$5\,m capture volume used in these experiments.

The MoCap-to-PX4 interface pipeline---including coordinate frame transformations (ENU~$\to$~NED~$\to$~FRD), the \texttt{mocap\_px4\_bridge} node, and EKF2 fusion with the onboard 250\,Hz IMU---is described in \autoref{sec:coordinate_frames} (Methodology). The EKF2 output is validated against MoCap ground truth in that same section, and its inertial bridging behavior during impact is explicitly demonstrated.

The physical experiment environment and the automated flight pipeline that produced these 127 verified collisions are described in the following subsections.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\subsubsection{Experiment Environment and Collision Target}
The physical experiments were conducted at the REALLAB at the IT University of Copenhagen (ITU) within an OptiTrack Motion Capture (MoCap) environment. A vertically oriented cylindrical cardboard column, internally reinforced with an aluminum profile, was anchored to the floor and ceiling to serve as the static collision target. The column oscillates slightly when subjected to manual force, but no additional structural supports were added in order to prevent any occlusion of the MoCap cameras, preserving their function as the primary reference point for the drone's spatial positioning. Analysis of the MoCap data confirmed that obstacle displacement during impacts was below the system's millimetre tracking resolution. Therefore, energy absorption by the environment is considered negligible.

\subsubsection{Experiment Control and Data Collection}

The flight stack (PX4, EKF2, ROS~2, Micro-XRCE-DDS, \texttt{flight\_director.py}, and the MoCap--PX4 coordinate frame integration) is described in \autoref{sec:methodology}. The telemetry recording pipeline (\texttt{flight\_recorder.py} $\to$ segmented \texttt{.mcap} files $\to$ Python database pipeline) and the manual IMU-based impact vetting procedure are also covered there. \subsubsection{Spatial Trajectory and Recovery Metrics}
\label{sec:clearance_sdld}

The spatial consequences of the physical impacts are quantified by assessing the clearance, the worst-case deviation, and the total recovery area. These values are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:spatial_metrics}.

\textbf{Obstacle clearance} represents the closest physical approach. The rotating cage exhibits a larger mean negative clearance, meaning it incurs deeper into the column boundary (Rotating: $\mu = -0.637 \pm 0.172$~m; Fixed: $\mu = -0.470 \pm 0.165$~m). The rotating cage achieves deeper clearance than the fixed cage, consistent with the bearing mechanism allowing the outer shell to roll past the obstacle rather than scrape against it.

\textbf{Maximum Trajectory Deviation} ($d_{\text{max}}$) is the peak perpendicular distance between the executed MoCap track and the commanded `ideal' reference line. This ideal line is drawn exactly from the coordinate where the drone was commanded forward (\texttt{WP1}) to the exact target coordinate it must reach (\texttt{WP2}, which includes a 15~cm precision leeway). This metric captures the single worst-case excursion during the obstacle transit. According to Table~\ref{tab:spatial_metrics}, the rotating cage recorded a maximum trajectory deviation of $131.0 \pm 32.1$~mm, while the fixed cage recorded $118.4 \pm 30.4$~mm.



\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Spatial Integral of Absolute Error (SIAE).}
SIAE measures the total spatial area footprint of the recovery phase: it integrates the absolute perpendicular deviation from the commanded path over the entire post-impact recovery window, capturing both the duration and the magnitude of the drift. A brief large excursion and a prolonged small offset can therefore produce similar SIAE values---the metric reflects the cumulative burden placed on the flight controller rather than any single worst-case instant.

\textbf{Path Spread (RMSLD).}
RMSLD (Root Mean Square Lateral Deviation) quantifies how tightly the drone tracks the ideal line over the entirety of the obstacle passage. It is the RMS of the perpendicular distance between the executed MoCap track and the commanded reference line, evaluated from the approach through the exit waypoint.
\end{tcolorbox}

Table~\ref{tab:spatial_metrics} reports these values: the rotating cage recorded a SIAE of $120.4 \pm 32.2 \times 10^{-3}~\text{m}^2$ and an RMSLD of $40.3 \pm 9.8$~mm; the fixed cage recorded $117.4 \pm 33.2 \times 10^{-3}~\text{m}^2$ and $37.4 \pm 11.0$~mm. To visualize the aggregate consistency across all flight tests, the overlaid paths of all flights are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:path_heatmap}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plot_16_path_heatmap.png}
    \caption{Path overlay of all impact flights for both cage configurations. Each flight trajectory is plotted at low opacity, showing the aggregate spatial footprint of the collision sweep. The dashed line marks the nominal commanded path.}
    \label{fig:path_heatmap}
\end{figure}

Based on the data reported above, the spatial recovery metrics suggest that the rotating cage's bearing mechanism does not expand the final recovery footprint required by the standard flight controller settings. This pattern is further evaluated by comparing the worst-case excursions and recovery consistency across configurations.

The two complementary metrics---maximum deviation and SIAE---confirm that a brief large excursion and a prolonged small offset can produce similar SIAE values. The rotating cage exhibited a slightly wider maximum trajectory deviation than the fixed cage. While this difference is statistically significant ($U = 2484$, $p = 0.022$, Cohen's $d = 0.41$), the effect size is small. More importantly, the total spatial area required by the Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controller to arrest the drift and return to the setpoint (SIAE) remains nearly \mbox{identical} between the two configurations ($U = 2009$, $p = 0.998$, not significant). \textbf{In summary, the rotating cage is the better overall configuration for spatial recovery:} it achieves deeper obstacle clearance with a nearly identical recovery footprint, and the small increase in maximum deviation is absorbed by the PID controller without expanding the total recovery envelope.

\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Huber Regression.}
Huber regression is a robust linear technique used throughout this thesis to model noisy crash telemetry. Unlike ordinary least squares, which squares every error equally and is easily skewed by a single extreme spike, Huber regression applies a squared penalty to small residuals but switches to a linear penalty for large ones---down-weighting the influence of violent transient outliers that are common in collision data. In Table~\ref{tab:deceleration_battery} the near-zero Huber slopes confirm that battery state-of-charge is not systematically correlated with impact deceleration; the technique reappears in \autoref{sec:angle_prediction} as a single-feature linear baseline against which the Random Forest is benchmarked.
\end{tcolorbox}


\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Cohen's $d$ (Effect Size).}
Cohen's $d$ is the standardised mean difference between two groups:
\begin{equation}
    d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s_{\text{pooled}}}
\end{equation}
where $s_{\text{pooled}}$ is the pooled standard deviation. Unlike a $p$-value, which only answers ``is there a difference?'', $d$ answers ``how large is the difference?'' in units of standard deviations. Conventional benchmarks: $|d| \approx 0.2$ = small, $0.5$ = medium, $0.8$ = large.

\textbf{Statistical Tests Used.}
\textbf{Welch's $t$-test} (reported as $t(\text{df})$, $p$) compares the means of two independent groups without assuming equal variance. The \textbf{Mann--Whitney $U$ test} (reported as $U$, $p$) is its non-parametric counterpart: it compares distributions by rank rather than by raw values, making no assumption of normality. Throughout this thesis, $U$ is used for spatial and saturation metrics (which tend to be skewed or zero-inflated), while $t$ is used for normally distributed measures such as deceleration magnitudes.
\end{tcolorbox}

The mechanical trade-off encoded in these three dimensions is this: a collision-mitigating mechanism can either \emph{absorb} the energy locally (keeping it out of the airframe), \emph{redirect} it into a less destructive axis, or \emph{survive} it by maintaining control authority. The rows in Table~\ref{tab:master_comparison} reveal which strategy each cage configuration favours, and whether the bearing mechanism trades one form of protection for another.

\medskip
The preceding kinematic and vibrational analyses establish that the rotating cage fundamentally alters the physical distribution of collision energy. However, for a drone operating without external motion-tracking, this mechanical advantage is only practically useful if the onboard sensors retain enough information to characterise the impact. As indicated by the IMU feature correlation analysis (see \autoref{fig:imu_correlation}), the relationship between impact angle and inertial response is highly complex and dispersed across multiple axes. This dispersion suggests that simple linear thresholds are insufficient, necessitating a multivariate, non-linear approach to successfully decode the collision geometry from the raw telemetry.

\subsection{Intermediate Mechanical Discussion: Energy Deflection and Survivability}
\label{sec:intermediate_discussion}
% ======================================================================

To accurately assess the structural and aerodynamic consequences of the obstacle collisions, the raw telemetry generated during the 400~ms contact window must be translated into measurable physical kinematics. Table~\ref{tab:master_comparison} summarises these refined comparative metrics, isolating the mechanical influence of the freely rotating cage against the rigid baseline.

Before interpreting the aggregate behaviour, it is necessary to define the mathematical basis of the primary metrics used to evaluate impact severity, as raw instantaneous peak values fail to capture the temporal realities of a crash.

\medskip
\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Accumulated Shock Impulse ($\Delta V$).}
Rather than recording a single instantaneous spike, impact severity is quantified by integrating the absolute acceleration over the entire 400~ms contact window:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Impulse} = \int_{t_{\text{impact}}-0.05}^{t_{\text{impact}}+0.35} |a(t)| \; dt
\end{equation}
By taking the absolute value before integrating, this metric prevents oscillating vibrations from cancelling each other out. It captures the total shock impulse sustained across each axis, representing the cumulative physical ``energy dose'' absorbed by the airframe rather than net velocity lost.

\textbf{Attitude Rate RMS.}
The root-mean-square (RMS) of the angular velocity---Roll, Pitch, and Yaw rates---measures the sustained rotational turbulence transmitted to the chassis over the collision window. RMS is a standard measure of the effective magnitude of a varying signal: squaring the instantaneous values weights larger deviations more heavily, the mean captures the average power, and the square root returns the result to the original units.
\end{tcolorbox}
\medskip

\paragraph{Allocator Saturation Duration.}
This metric calculates the total time the flight controller's motor mixer is forced to hold actuator outputs at absolute hardware limits (0\% or 100\%) while attempting to arrest collision-induced drift. The distribution of saturation durations for both cage configurations is shown in \autoref{fig:allocator_saturation} (Annex).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The 3D Kinematic Trade-Off (X, Y, and Z Axes).}
The shock impulse metrics reveal a distinct divergence in how kinetic energy propagates through the drone depending on the cage configuration. The rotating cage excels at lateral shock absorption, reducing the primary collision impulse on the Y-axis by 38.3\% compared to the fixed cage. By rolling across the obstacle rather than scraping, it prevents a severe lateral bounce-back.

However, this lateral deflection carries a vertical cost. The sudden redirection of lateral momentum by the bearing mechanism couples a portion of that energy into the vertical axis via axial loading through the bearing race. This results in an 11.8\% increase in vertical shock impulse (Z-axis). The fixed cage, conversely, absorbs the energy through whole-chassis deformation---it ``slumps'' against the column, taking more lateral damage but dampening the instantaneous vertical transfer.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Rotational Decoupling (Roll/Pitch vs.\ Yaw).}
The most striking evidence of the rotating cage's mechanical advantage is found in the Attitude Control metrics, which highlight the specific directional limits of the bearing. The bearing spins freely around the vertical Z-axis; consequently, the Yaw Rate RMS drops by a massive 85\% (from 0.57 to 0.09~rad/s). The bearing effectively decouples the rotational friction of the wall from the flight controller's IMU.

However, the cage is mechanically constrained and cannot spin freely around the X (Roll) or Y (Pitch) axes. When the drone hits the column, the forward kinetic energy still violently twists the airframe. As a result, the Roll and Pitch Rate RMS are elevated for the rotating cage (+36.2\% and +13.4\%, respectively). The drone still twists, but because the yaw axis is completely liberated, the flight controller is not overwhelmed by multi-axis friction.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Hardware Limits and System Survivability.}
Ultimately, surviving a confined-space collision is a race against gravity dictated by hardware limits. The fixed cage forces the drone to fight wall friction across all three rotational axes while simultaneously battling a massive lateral rebound. This overwhelms the flight controller, forcing it to command maximum output from the motors, holding them at absolute saturation for a mean of 134~ms (with severe unpredictability, as some flights exceeded 300~ms)---an eternity in flight dynamics.

By perfectly isolating the yaw axis and absorbing the lateral impulse, the rotating cage drastically simplifies the aerodynamic recovery. Even though the drone still experiences elevated roll and pitch turbulence, the reduction in total chaotic friction reduces mean motor saturation time to just 5~ms. The system remains well within its operational envelope, allowing for immediate aerodynamic recovery and continued flight.

This systematic deflection of collision energy fundamentally alters the relationship between impact geometry and the onboard IMU response. The following section evaluates whether the vibrational signature recorded by the flight controller's sensors still retains enough information to recover the impact angle, or whether the bearing's decoupling disperses the signal beyond recognition.

\subsection{Feature Extraction: Correlating IMU Telemetry to the Impact Angle (RQ2)}
\label{sec:result_analysis}
% ======================================================================

This section addresses the second research question (RQ2): whether the onboard IMU telemetry recorded during a collision can be used to infer the physical impact angle without external ground truth. The 26 IMU-derived aggregate features introduced in the methodology (\autoref{sec:methodology}) are correlated against the MoCap-derived impact angle to determine which inertial channels carry angle-discriminative information, and to what extent that information is preserved across the two cage configurations.



\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Pearson's Correlation Coefficient ($r$).}
$r$ measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables, ranging from $-1$ (perfect negative correlation) to $+1$ (perfect positive correlation), with $0$ indicating no linear relationship. Concretely, $r$ quantifies how consistently two variables move together relative to their individual variability: if impact angle and a given IMU feature tend to rise and fall in lockstep, their Pearson $r$ will be far from zero; if they fluctuate independently, $r$ will be near zero. (Mathematically, $r$ is the covariance of the two variables divided by the product of their standard deviations, normalising the result to the $[-1,1]$ interval.)

\textbf{Statistical Significance ($p$).}
The $p$-value indicates the probability that the observed correlation occurred by chance: $^{***} p < 0.001$ (highly significant, less than 0.1\% probability of a spurious result), $^{**} p < 0.01$ (strongly significant, less than 1\%), and $^{*} p < 0.05$ (significant, less than 5\%). Formal mathematical derivations are provided in the Math Annex (\autoref{sec:math_appendix}).
\end{tcolorbox}

The consolidated feature correlation slopegraph (\autoref{fig:imu_correlation}) shows which sensor channels are most useful for predicting the impact angle.

    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plots/consolidated_feature_correlation.png}

\end{figure}

Parallel coordinates (\autoref{fig:consolidated_parallel_coordinates}) show the same picture from another angle. The top 10 IMU features for the Rotating Cage---ranked by how strongly each correlates with impact angle in that configuration---are shown for both cage types (the same features are plotted for the Fixed Cage to enable direct comparison). Each coloured line represents one flight, normalised so all features are on the same scale. The lines from different angle bins (colours) cross and overlap across every axis---no single feature cleanly separates shallow impacts from steep ones. This confirms that the impact angle information is spread across many IMU channels, which is why a single-feature model cannot work and a multi-feature model like Random Forest is needed.

\subsubsection{Angle Prediction via Random Forest}
\label{sec:angle_prediction}

To map these complex, dispersed vibrational signatures to a specific impact angle, a Random Forest regressor was employed. Random Forest is an ensemble machine learning algorithm that constructs multiple decision trees during training and outputs the mean prediction of those individual trees. It is particularly well-suited for this application because it natively models non-linear relationships and complex interactions between multiple variables---such as the coupled gyroscopic and acceleration forces generated during a multi-axis collision. Furthermore, unlike strict linear models, it does not require the underlying physical data to follow a formal mathematical distribution, making it highly effective at parsing chaotic, high-frequency crash telemetry.

Model performance is evaluated using three standard regression metrics to provide a holistic view of predictive accuracy.


\begin{tcolorbox}[breakable, colback=gray!8, colframe=black!30, arc=0pt,
    boxsep=10pt, left=4pt, right=4pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
    before skip=6pt, after skip=6pt]
\textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE).}
The average absolute difference between the predicted and actual impact angles, providing a direct, easily interpretable measure of typical angular error in degrees. An MAE of 5$^\circ$ means the model's predictions are, on average, $\pm$5$^\circ$ from the true value.

\textbf{Root Mean Square Error (RMSE).}
Similar to MAE but squares each error before averaging and then takes the square root; this mathematically penalizes larger deviations more heavily. A substantial gap between MAE and RMSE indicates the presence of severe individual mispredictions---errors that are large in magnitude but infrequent.

\textbf{Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$).}
$R^2$ quantifies the proportion of variance in the true impact angle that the IMU features successfully explain. An $R^2$ of 1 means the model accounts for all variability in the data; 0 means it performs no better than always predicting the mean; negative values (possible in cross-validation or transfer evaluation) mean the model is worse than the mean baseline.
\end{tcolorbox}

While the preceding sections characterise the mechanical consequences of collision, they rely on post-processed MoCap ground truth. A practical drone operating in a confined space does not have access to an external tracking system --- it must infer collision properties from onboard sensors alone. To evaluate whether impact angle can be estimated from the vibration and inertial signatures recorded by the onboard IMU, a Random Forest regressor was trained on the 14 IMU-derived features described in the methodology (\autoref{sec:methodology}). The model was evaluated separately for each cage configuration using stratified 5-fold cross-validation, enabling direct comparison of how well the vibration-to-angle mapping survives the mechanical differences between fixed and rotating designs.

\subsubsection{Five-Fold Cross-Validation: Predicted vs.\ Actual Impact Angle}

The 5-fold cross-validation procedure splits the impact flights for each cage configuration into five subgroups, trains the model on four, and evaluates on the held-out fifth in rotation. This ensures every flight contributes to testing exactly once, providing an unbiased estimate of generalisation performance on unseen data.

Both configurations produced predictive performance substantially above chance, though with distinct error profiles (Table~\ref{tab:rf_cv_metrics}, \autoref{fig:rf_cv_consolidated}). \begin{figure}[htbp]

The learning curves for both cage configurations (Figure~\ref{fig:rf_learning_consolidated}) exhibit a clear overfitting signature: a persistent gap of approximately 0.10 between the training $R^2$ (around 0.80) and the validation $R^2$ (around 0.70). A gap this wide means the model is capturing noise patterns in the training set that do not transfer to new flights. While the validation $R^2$ of $\sim$0.70 confirms meaningful predictive power, the size of the training--validation gap implies that performance would benefit either from acquiring additional training examples, from stronger regularisation that constrains the model's capacity to memorise, or from reducing model complexity to better match the effective information content of the 14-feature input space.

\subsubsection{Model Comparison Against Linear Baseline}

The Random Forest succeeded in recovering the impact angle from IMU data alone. But a critical sanity check remains: could a simple linear regression on the single best-correlated feature have achieved the same result? To answer this, the model was benchmarked against a Huber linear regressor operating on the single best-correlated IMU feature for each configuration.

To establish this baseline for comparison, Huber regression (described in \autoref{sec:impact_acceleration_vibrations}) was applied to the top three individually best-correlated IMU features for each cage configuration. \autoref{fig:eda_top3_scatter} shows the raw data: each subplot plots one IMU feature against the measured impact angle, with the Huber trendline superimposed. If any single IMU channel carried a strong linear relationship to impact angle, the points would cluster tightly around the trendline and the line would have a clear slope.

    \centering

These represent the best-case linear scenario: the single strongest individual sensor channel available. The wide scatter and flat slopes, particularly for the Rotating Cage, visually confirm why single-feature linear regression cannot recover the impact angle.}

They do not. In both cage types the points are broadly dispersed and the trendlines are nearly flat, meaning even the best single sensor channel cannot reliably distinguish a shallow ($\sim$30$^\circ$) impact from a steep ($\sim$60$^\circ$) one. The Rotating Cage is especially severe: the bearing mechanism disperses the angle signature so thoroughly across multiple vibrational modes that no individual feature retains a usable linear trace. \autoref{fig:rf_huber_consolidated} quantifies this collapse in a single number by comparing each configuration's best-feature Huber $R^2$ against the full 14-feature Random Forest.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

\end{figure}

The comparison reveals a striking asymmetry between the two cage types. For the Fixed Cage, the single-feature Huber baseline achieves an $R^2$ of 0.348 --- weak but containing genuine correlation. The Random Forest lifts this to $R^2 = 0.731$, a gain of $\Delta R^2 = +0.384$ attributable to its ability to combine non-linear interactions across all 14 features.

For the Rotating Cage, the pattern is fundamentally different. The Huber baseline collapses to an $R^2$ of 0.004 --- functionally zero, indicating that no single linear IMU channel carries usable angle information. Yet the Random Forest recovers an $R^2$ of 0.719 from the same data. The $\Delta R^2 = +0.714$ gain is nearly double the Fixed Cage improvement, suggesting that the bearing mechanism distributes the angle signature across multiple IMU channels rather than concentrating it in one dominant axis---a signal structure that only a multi-feature model can exploit. This is a plausible mechanical consequence of decoupling the contact surface from the airframe: by converting a rigid scrape into a rolling contact, the rotating cage may spread the collision energy across a broader set of vibrational modes rather than channelling it along a single structural path. However, confirming this causal mechanism would require complementary measurement (e.g., high-speed footage or bearing torque instrumentation) that was not available in the present setup; the Random Forest results demonstrate the \emph{existence} of a distributed multivariate signal, not its physical origin.

\subsubsection{Cross-Condition Transfer: Are the Dynamics Transferable?}

A critical practical question is whether a model trained on one cage type can perform on the other --- i.e., whether the learned vibration-to-angle mapping generalises across mechanical configurations.

\subsubsection{Feature Importance: Which IMU Channels Carry the Signal?}

To understand which physical signatures the models rely on, the permutation-based feature importance was examined for both configurations (Figure~\ref{fig:consolidated_feature_importance}). Permutation importance measures the drop in $R^2$ when a single feature's values are randomly shuffled, breaking its association with the target while preserving its marginal distribution. A larger drop indicates a more critical feature.

\medskip
Together, these results confirm that standard flight controller IMUs carry sufficient information for tactile angle estimation, and that the rotating cage disperses rather than destroys this signal. However, the failure of the cross-condition transfer explicitly demonstrates that this predictive mapping is highly non-linear and deeply platform-specific.
