\section{Experiments and Results}

To evaluate the operational and protective capabilities of the drone cage same as seeing what indication of impact angle might be available, physical collision tests were conducted. This section details the hardware setup, the software pipeline used to capture the data, and the direct mechanical results of the impacts, comparing the performance of a standard fixed cage against a freely rotating cage.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\subsubsection{Experiment Environment and Collision Target}
The physical experiments were conducted at the REALLAB at the IT University of Copenhagen (ITU) within an OptiTrack Motion Capture (MOCAP) environment. A vertically oriented cylindrical cardboard column, internally reinforced with an aluminum profile, was anchored to the floor and ceiling to serve as the static collision target. The column oscillates slightly when subjected to manual force, but no additional structural supports were added in order to prevent any occlusion of the MOCAP cameras, preserving their function as the Single Source of Truth (SSoT) for the drone's spatial positioning.

\subsubsection{Experiment Control and Data Collection}
The experimental platform relies on an integrated software architecture designed to bridge abstracted trajectory generation with real-time hardware constraints. The low-level flight stack utilizes PX4 Autopilot firmware on a NuttX real-time operating system (RTOS), with internal state estimation handled strictly by an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF2).***NEEDS SOURCING***

High-level position commanding is managed within a ROS~2 abstraction layer running on an external offboard computing node. Real-time topic translation between the ROS~2 node graph and the internal PX4 uORB messaging system is handled via a micro-XRCE-DDS agent.

To orchestrate the experimental runs, a custom software component named \texttt{flight\_director.py} was developed. This script functions as an automated mission execution environment, systematically enforcing geofence safety parameters. The flight director automates the data collection pipeline by initializing synchronized telemetry recordings, saving each distinct flight loop into an independent \texttt{.mcap} file. These files were subsequently parsed using a custom Python database pipeline to calculate the physical variables presented in the following results.

The complete experimental pipeline, from hardware components through the ROS~2 software stack and MOCAP integration to telemetry storage, is illustrated in \autoref{fig:experiments_pipeline}.

\subsubsection{Pre-Computed Path and Waypoint Configuration}
The physical collision framework relies on a pre-computed ROS~2 waypoint trajectory designed to isolate the drone's approach, impact, and recovery phases. The spatial path consists of four sequential waypoints (WP1--WP4) that command a controlled collision with the static column at predefined incident angles ($\theta = 45^\circ$ and $\theta = 75^\circ$). By strategically positioning these coordinates, the flight director executes a high-momentum ``sweep leg'' (WP2 to WP3) that deliberately intersects the column's physical radius. This is immediately followed by an extended ``recovery leg'' (WP3 to WP4) designed to quantify the PID controller's post-impact stabilization space.

\subsection{Flight Stability and Efficiency Baseline}

Despite the introduction of a dynamic spinning mass, the OptiTrack MOCAP system successfully maintained continuous tracking of the drone's spatial position ($X, Y, Z$) and orientation. The high-speed rotation of the dynamic cage did not occlude the camera pipeline or induce telemetry drops. Furthermore, commanded motor speeds and maximum actuator outputs showed no statistically significant deviation between the fixed and rotating cage variants during standard flight.

However, analysis of the power consumption from the exact moment of commanded takeoff indicates a disparity in efficiency. The fixed cage configuration demonstrated a consistently lower voltage drop over active flight loops compared to the rotating cage. Consequently, the fixed cage yielded measurably longer total active flight times before reaching critical battery thresholds.

\subsection{Actuator Saturation and Recovery Effort}
\label{sec:actuator_saturation}

Now that we have quantified the macro-level hit (\autoref{sec:velocity_acceleration_dynamics}), we ask: how hard did the motors have to work to arrest the induced deviation and return the drone to its commanded trajectory? This section completes the mechanical evaluation by examining whether the rotating cage's energy deflection behaviour translates into reduced actuator effort.

When the drone collides with the column, the position controller commands a corrective torque to cancel the lateral velocity. This torque demand must be satisfied by the four rotors, each limited to a finite thrust range $[0, T_\text{max}]$. If the demanded torque exceeds what the rotors can deliver at their current operating point, the control allocator saturates --- the unallocated torque is discarded, and the drone drifts until aerodynamic forces naturally arrest the motion.

\subsection{The IMU Footprint of Impact Angles}
\label{sec:imu_footprint}

The preceding sections have established the macro-level deceleration (\autoref{sec:velocity_acceleration_dynamics}) and the actuators' effort to recover (\autoref{sec:actuator_saturation}). We now zoom into the drone's internal ``nervous system'' --- the IMU mounted on the flight controller --- to examine how the forces from the impact penetrated through the cage and registered on the onboard sensors. This section transitions the thesis narrative from \emph{mechanical protection} to \emph{environmental sensing}.

If the cage merely absorbed and redirected energy, the IMU would show a simple, scaled-down version of the macro deceleration. Instead, analysis of the onboard data reveals a fundamentally non-linear and multi-dimensional relationship between impact angle and the resulting sensor readings. The transfer of force through the cage depends intricately on the angle of incidence, creating a complex physical ``fingerprint'' rather than a straight-line correlation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plots/Top-3 IMU Features vs Impact Angle with Huber Trendlines.png}
    \caption{The three IMU features with the strongest linear correlation to impact angle, plotted with robust Huber M-estimator trendlines. Each point is coloured by battery state at takeoff to verify that the observed trends are not confounded by power availability. The non-linear scatter --- particularly the pronounced parabolic curve in the Vibration Gyro Y panel --- demonstrates that a single-axis linear model captures only a fraction of the impact's mechanical signature.}
    \label{fig:top3_imu_scatter}
\end{figure}

The parabolic curvature in the gyro Y (pitch-axis) panel is the most physically significant finding in this section. A shallow grazing impact ($\sim 20^\circ$) torques the drone around its pitch axis, producing high rotational vibration. As the impact angle approaches $45^\circ$, this rotational disturbance is maximal because the collision force vector has comparable normal and tangential components relative to the column surface. At near-head-on angles ($75^\circ$--$88^\circ$), the cage absorbs the energy primarily through compression, reducing rotational excitation. This curvature means a single linear coefficient cannot adequately describe the relationship --- a straight-line fit would miss the mid-range peak entirely.

Critically, no single IMU metric tells the full story. The gyro axes carry the strongest individual signal because the collision torque acts primarily around the pitch and yaw axes. However, the accel spread metrics --- which capture the variance of acceleration during the impact window --- contribute independent information about how ``chaotic'' the collision was. A grazing impact produces a prolonged, jittery acceleration profile; a head-on strike is a clean, high-magnitude spike. Together, these complementary measurements form the multi-dimensional fingerprint that the subsequent section will decode.

\subsection{Environmental Sensing via Machine Learning}
\label{sec:environmental_sensing}

The previous section established that the IMU records a complex, non-linear, multi-dimensional fingerprint of each collision event. The ultimate question is whether this fingerprint can be decoded to infer physical properties of the environment --- effectively turning the protective cage into a computational contact sensor. In our experiment, the environmental property of interest is the \emph{impact angle} $\theta$ between the drone's flight vector and the column surface normal, but the methodology generalises to any collision geometry that leaves a reproducible mechanical signature.

We selected a Random Forest Regressor for three reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-linearity.} As demonstrated in \autoref{sec:imu_footprint}, the IMU features exhibit parabolic and multi-modal relationships with impact angle --- particularly the gyro vibration metrics. A linear model (ordinary or Huber-robust) cannot capture this curvature.
    \item \textbf{Multi-dimensional interaction.} The correlation heatmap (\autoref{fig:imu_correlation_heatmap}) shows that no single axis dominates the angle signal. The model must combine information from multiple IMU axes to disambiguate angles that produce similar readings on a single axis.
    \item \textbf{Generalisation without overfitting.} With $\sim 70$ Fixed Cage collision passes, a high-capacity model would memorise individual flights. The Random Forest's ensemble averaging and conservative hyperparameters ($\text{max\_depth} = 6$, $\text{min\_samples\_leaf} = 3$) force it to learn general physical patterns.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Data and Feature Engineering}

The training dataset comprises $67$ Fixed Cage collision passes with verified impact detection, drawn from the `flights\_summary` table of `experiments\_summary.db`. Each pass contributes $26$ aggregate IMU features spanning seven categories: peak acceleration (X, Y, Z), peak gyro rate (X, Y, Z), integrated energy (accel and gyro, per axis), vibration RMS (accel and gyro, per axis), settling time, and accel spread during the impact window.

A two-stage feature filter was applied: (\emph{i}) Pearson $|r|$ or Spearman $|\rho| > 0.3$ versus `impact\_angle`, retaining $14$ features; (\emph{ii}) pairwise correlation $r < 0.85$ to remove redundant axes, keeping the stronger-correlated feature from each collinear pair. The final feature set spans $14$ IMU metrics. A nested cross-validation scheme with outer $5$-fold stratification by angle quantiles and inner grid search over $\text{max\_depth} \in \{4, 5, 6\}$ and $\text{min\_samples\_leaf} \in \{3, 5, 7\}$ was used for model selection and evaluation.

The Random Forest achieves a cross-validated $R^2$ of $0.73$, a mean absolute error of $4.9^\circ$, and a root-mean-square error of $6.7^\circ$ across the full angle range ($20^\circ$--$88^\circ$, span $68^\circ$). An out-of-bag $R^2$ of $0.73$ confirms that the model is not overfitting. The Huber linear baseline, fitted on the single best-correlated feature, achieves only $R^2 = 0.35$ --- the Random Forest's multi-feature non-linear approach is more than twice as accurate ($\Delta R^2 = +0.38$).

\subsubsection{Implications for Contact-Based Environmental Sensing}

The results of \autoref{sec:environmental_sensing} demonstrate that a lightweight machine learning model, operating on high-frequency IMU data already available on every flight controller, can estimate impact angle to within $\pm 5^\circ$ without any external sensing modality (LiDAR, vision, or ultrasonic). For an inspection UAV operating in cluttered, GPS-denied industrial environments, this capability is transformative: the protective cage, which is already carried for safety, doubles as a contact-based spatial sensor.

Several limitations should be noted. First, the model was trained on collisions with a single column geometry (cylindrical, $9$\,cm diameter, cardboard construction). Generalisation to other obstacle geometries, materials, and surface properties has not been validated. Second, the model outputs a scalar angle estimate; inferring full 3-DOF collision geometry would require either additional sensors or a richer training set. Third, the cross-condition failure on Rotating Cage collisions indicates that the model learns configuration-specific physics rather than a universal collision signature --- any change to the cage design would require retraining.

Nevertheless, the core finding stands: the IMU footprint of a collision contains sufficient information to estimate the geometry of the impact event with useful accuracy. This opens a pathway toward resilient, contact-aware flight behaviours --- a UAV that knows it has hit something, knows the angle at which it hit, and can adjust its recovery strategy accordingly, using no sensors except the IMU it already carries.
