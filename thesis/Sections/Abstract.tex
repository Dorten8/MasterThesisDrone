\begin{abstract}

Navigating confined, GPS-denied environments presents a severe collision risk for autonomous UAVs. This thesis investigates whether a freely rotating, decoupled spherical cage can deflect impact energy to improve survivability, and whether the resulting inertial telemetry can be exploited to characterize the collision geometry. A custom quadcopter was evaluated across 127 motion-captured collisions, isolating the cage's bearing mechanism as the sole independent variable. Kinematic results demonstrate that the rotating cage significantly reduces peak impact deceleration by converting linear momentum into rotational energy. Furthermore, to overcome the chaotic noise inherent in crash telemetry, a Random Forest regressor was applied to the raw Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) signatures. The model demonstrated the capability to extract the collision angle, significantly outperforming linear baselines despite a constrained dataset size. These findings prove that mechanical deflection enhances structural survivability, and that non-linear machine learning can extract tactical spatial data from standard flight controllers, potentially enabling ``navigation by contact'' without specialized exteroceptive sensors.

\end{abstract}
